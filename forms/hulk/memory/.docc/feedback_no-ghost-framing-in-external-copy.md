In external/investor/marketing copy, do not invoke substrate-ghost or AI-ecosystem framing for how a duo achieves studio-scale output. Substrate-ghost vocabulary (ghosts/<slug>/, commissioned-agent organizations, biographer/code-carrier/orchestrator entities) reads as the founder *being* a substrate-ghost or disappearing into an AI ecosystem, rather than as a named human using AI tools.

**Why:** On 2026-05-18, while drafting Speedrun investor copy, rismay corrected: "don't mention rismay as a ghost." The previous draft's *"plus an AI ecosystem that handles the operational reach of a studio with the headcount of a duo"* read as ghost-coded — even though it was meant to point at the commissioned-agent setup, an investor reads it as "this founder is so deep in the AI-substrate that he's not a normal operator anymore." That's a credibility hit. Investors fund people who *use* AI as tooling; they get cautious about people who *are* an AI ecosystem.

**How to apply:**
- For the leverage explanation (how a duo ships at studio scale), use concrete tooling language: *"with AI tooling tuned so a duo ships at studio scale"* / *"running a tightly-tuned engineering pipeline that lets a duo carry studio output"*.
- Avoid in external copy: "AI ecosystem," "commissioned agents," "agent organization," "biographer/code-carrier/orchestrator" by name, "ghost-shell," anything that names individual substrate agents as if they're co-workers.
- Founders are named humans (Cristian Monterroza + John). The substrate is *engineering infrastructure they built*, not *entities they co-found with*.
- This is a sharpening of the parent value-driven-over-technical-framing rule. That rule says drop substrate vocabulary; this rule says specifically that AI-ecosystem/agent-soup framing is the most damaging kind of substrate vocabulary in investor copy, because it reads as eccentric beyond just being technical.
- For internal/engineering peer/substrate doctrine work, agent-ecosystem framing remains correct and load-bearing.
